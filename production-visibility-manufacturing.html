Why On-Time Delivery Suffers

Real-time production visibility for manufacturers is a system that connects ERP, scheduling, and shop floor data into a unified view of every active job's status, progress, and delivery risk. The system flags jobs that are falling behind before they miss their ship date, giving production managers the time and information to intervene.

On-time delivery (OTD) in job shop manufacturing averages 83% to 87% across the industry. That number masks the reality. Most shops measure OTD against the last confirmed promise date, which often reflects a date that has already been pushed back once or twice. Measured against the original promise date from the initial order acknowledgment, OTD at many shops drops to 70% to 78%.

Late delivery costs compound in ways that do not appear on any single report. The direct costs are visible: expediting charges, premium freight, overtime to recover. A single air freight shipment on a late aerospace order can cost $2,000 to $8,000 depending on weight and destination. The indirect costs are larger and harder to trace. Customers who experience repeated late deliveries reduce their order volume, shift work to competitors, or remove the shop from their approved supplier list entirely. That revenue loss happens gradually and is rarely attributed back to the production visibility problem that caused it.

The root cause, in most job shops, is the same. The production manager cannot see what is actually happening across all active jobs in real time. The information exists. It is scattered across systems that do not communicate.

Data Silos: ERP, Scheduling, and Floor Systems

A typical 50 to 150-person job shop runs three categories of systems that each hold a piece of the production picture. None of them, on their own, shows the full view.

The ERP Layer

JobBOSS, Epicor, ProShop, Global Shop Solutions, or a similar system manages orders, job records, material purchasing, and invoicing. The ERP knows what was ordered, what the routing is, what materials are needed, and when the job is supposed to ship. It does not know where the job is right now on the shop floor, whether the machine it needs is running or down, or whether the material that was supposed to arrive Monday actually showed up.

The Scheduling Layer

Some shops use dedicated scheduling software (Schedlyzer, Jobpack, Visual Planning, or a module within their ERP). Others use Excel spreadsheets, whiteboards, or scheduling boards on the shop wall. The scheduling layer shows the plan: which jobs run on which machines in what sequence. It does not automatically update when the plan breaks down. A machine that goes down at 10 AM might not be reflected in the schedule until someone manually updates it, which might happen at 2 PM or the next morning.

The Floor Layer

What is actually happening. Machine status: running, idle, down for maintenance, waiting for setup. Job progress: operation 3 of 7 is 60% complete. Operator activity: first shift started the job, second shift is continuing it. Material availability: the bar stock is staged at the machine, or it is still on the receiving dock waiting to be inspected. This information lives in the heads of supervisors and operators, on paper travelers, and occasionally in a data collection terminal that feeds a system nobody checks in real time.

Where the Breaks Happen

The production manager sits in a meeting at 8 AM and reviews the schedule. Sixteen jobs are supposed to ship this week. The ERP says materials are in house for all of them. The schedule shows capacity is allocated. Everything looks on track.

By 11 AM, three things have changed. The Mazak Integrex has thrown an alarm and is waiting for a maintenance technician. The heat treat vendor called to say a batch will be 2 days late returning. And the quality department placed a hold on a lot of 6061 bar stock because the material cert does not match the purchase order. Three of the 16 jobs are now at risk of missing their ship date, but the production manager will not know this until they walk the floor, check their email, and make four phone calls, which might happen at 1 PM or might happen tomorrow morning if the day gets busy.

That gap between what changed and when the production manager learns about it is where late deliveries originate. Production visibility eliminates the gap.

What Production Visibility Actually Means

Production visibility is the ability to see the status of every active job, every machine, and every potential delivery risk from a single screen, updated continuously. The system does not require anyone to enter status updates manually (though it can accept them). It pulls data automatically from connected systems and calculates job progress, remaining work, and delivery probability in real time.

The difference between a production visibility system and a shop floor data collection system is intelligence. Data collection tells you what happened. Visibility tells you what is happening and what is about to happen if nothing changes. The AI component is what bridges the gap between data collection and predictive awareness.

A production manager using a visibility system sees a dashboard that answers five questions without clicking anything: Which jobs ship this week. Which of those are behind schedule. Why are they behind. What is the impact if they ship late. What action can be taken now to recover. The system answers these questions by synthesizing data from ERP, scheduling, floor status, equipment monitoring, and supplier communications into a single operational picture.

What a Production Dashboard Should Show

The production dashboard is the primary interface for the visibility system. Every element on the screen should answer a question that the production manager or shop owner asks daily.

Job Status View

Every active job listed with current operation, percent complete, scheduled ship date, and a risk indicator (on track, at risk, behind). The risk indicator is calculated, not manually assigned. If a job is at operation 4 of 8, with 3 days until the ship date, and the remaining operations historically take 4.5 days based on actual cycle times for this part type, the system flags it as at risk automatically.

Jobs are sortable by ship date, customer priority, risk level, and value. The production manager can view all 200 active jobs or filter to the 12 that need attention right now.

Machine Status View

Every machine displayed with current status: running (with job number and operator), idle, in setup, down for maintenance, or down with no assignment. Utilization percentage for the current shift. For machines connected through MTConnect or OPC-UA, cycle count and estimated time to operation completion.

This view answers the question: "Where is my capacity right now?" If the production manager needs to accelerate a job, they can see which machines are available, which operators are on shift, and which jobs can be bumped or rescheduled with the least downstream impact.

Delivery Risk Queue

A prioritized list of jobs at risk of missing their ship date, ranked by customer impact. Each entry includes the reason the job is at risk (machine down, material delay, quality hold, capacity conflict, outside processing delay), the estimated delay in hours or days, and suggested recovery actions. This is the screen the production manager opens first thing in the morning. On a good day, there are 3 to 5 items. On a bad day, 12 to 15. Either way, they are visible and actionable before the first phone call from a customer.

Customer Delivery Scorecard

On-time delivery performance by customer over 30, 60, and 90 days. This view answers: "Which customer relationships are we strengthening, and which are we putting at risk?" A customer with 96% OTD over the last 90 days is a relationship being maintained. A customer at 78% OTD is a relationship being damaged. The production manager can use this view to prioritize scheduling decisions when competing jobs require the same resource.

Material and Supplier Status

Outstanding purchase orders with expected delivery dates, cross-referenced against the jobs waiting for that material. If Supplier A's shipment of 17-4 PH stainless is 3 days late and four jobs depend on it, all four jobs appear in the delivery risk queue automatically. The system tracks supplier on-time delivery performance and factors it into risk calculations for future jobs.

How AI Surfaces Delivery Risk

The intelligence layer of a production visibility system uses historical production data to predict which jobs are likely to ship late, even when the current data suggests they are on track.

Pattern-Based Risk Detection

The system learns from your shop's production history. If jobs with more than 5 operations, involving outside heat treatment, and running on the Mazak Integrex historically take 22% longer than their scheduled time, the system applies that learning to every new job with similar characteristics. A job that appears on track based on the schedule might be flagged as moderate risk because the system knows this type of job typically runs over.

Cascading Delay Analysis

When one job falls behind, the system calculates the downstream impact. If Job A is delayed by 8 hours on Machine 3, and Machine 3 is scheduled to start Job B immediately after, the system flags both jobs and traces the impact through the entire schedule. In a tightly loaded shop, a single delay can cascade through 5 to 10 downstream jobs within 48 hours. Manual tracking cannot trace these cascades in real time. The system does it continuously.

Capacity Conflict Detection

The system compares the remaining work across all active jobs against available machine hours and operator hours for each shift. If the total remaining work exceeds the available capacity to complete it before the ship dates, the system identifies which jobs cannot physically be completed on time and surfaces them before the capacity crunch actually occurs. This gives the production manager a 3 to 5 day warning to add overtime, outsource operations, or negotiate revised ship dates with customers, rather than discovering the problem when it is too late to act.

Supplier Risk Scoring

The system tracks every purchase order and outside processing order against its promised delivery date. Over time, it builds a reliability score for each supplier. A heat treat vendor who delivers on time 72% of the time gets a different risk weighting than one who delivers at 95%. Jobs routed through lower-reliability suppliers carry higher risk scores automatically, giving the production manager earlier warning on the jobs most likely to be affected by supply chain delays.

Equipment Monitoring and Utilization

Equipment monitoring feeds directly into the production visibility system. Machine utilization is a key variable in delivery performance, and understanding it requires more than a general sense of how busy the shop looks.

Connecting to Machines

Modern CNC equipment from Mazak, DMG MORI, Haas, Okuma, and Doosan supports data output through MTConnect or OPC-UA protocols. These connections provide real-time cycle status, spindle load, feed rates, alarm codes, and program information. The connection is read-only and does not affect machine operation.

For older equipment without native connectivity, retrofit sensors provide a subset of data at low cost. A current sensor on the machine's main power circuit detects cycle start, cycle end, and idle states. Vibration sensors on the spindle housing track cutting conditions and flag anomalies. These sensors cost $200 to $800 per machine, install in under an hour, and provide 80% of the utilization data available from a full MTConnect connection.

What Utilization Data Reveals

Most job shops estimate their equipment utilization at 60% to 75%. Actual measured utilization, when tracked by the minute across a full week, typically comes in at 35% to 55%. The gap between perception and reality is large enough to have a material impact on delivery performance and capital spending decisions.

When you know that the Haas VF-6 runs at 41% utilization, you can investigate why. Setup time accounts for 18%. Waiting for material accounts for 12%. Unplanned downtime accounts for 8%. Scheduled maintenance accounts for 4%. The remaining 17% is idle time with no assigned reason. That 17% is recoverable capacity, equivalent to roughly 7 hours per day on a two-shift operation. Recovering even half of it through better scheduling and material staging is the equivalent of adding a $400,000 machine without buying one.

Utilization as a Delivery Lever

When a job is at risk of shipping late, the production manager needs to know where the capacity is to recover. The utilization data answers that question precisely. Machine 4 has 6 hours of available capacity on second shift this week. Machine 11 has 4 hours. Operator Martinez is qualified on both machines and available for overtime Thursday. The recovery plan writes itself when the data is visible. Without the data, the production manager makes phone calls, walks the floor, and guesses.

Data Architecture: Connecting the Systems

A production visibility system connects to three data layers. The architecture is designed to read from existing systems without modifying them.

ERP Connection

The system reads order data, job routings, material requirements, ship dates, and customer information from your ERP. For systems with API access (ProShop, Epicor Kinetic, Plex), the connection is real-time. For systems that use database queries (JobBOSS, Global Shop Solutions, IQMS), the system reads from the database on a configurable interval, typically every 5 to 15 minutes. The ERP is not modified. The connection is read-only.

Scheduling Connection

If the shop uses scheduling software, the system reads the current schedule and compares it against actual floor status. If the shop uses spreadsheets or whiteboards, the production visibility system can serve as the scheduling tool, generating recommended sequences based on ship dates, machine capacity, and material availability.

Floor Data Connection

Machine data comes through MTConnect adapters, OPC-UA servers, or retrofit sensors. Operator inputs (job started, job completed, quality hold, material issue) come through a simple terminal or tablet interface at each work center. The interface takes less than 10 seconds per entry. Some shops use barcode scanning against the job traveler to log operation completions. The system accepts whatever input method the shop's team will actually use consistently.

The three data streams converge in the visibility platform, which reconciles discrepancies (ERP says material is available, floor input says it has not arrived), calculates job progress against plan, and generates the risk assessments and dashboard views the production team uses daily.

Implementation

Implementing a production visibility system follows a phased approach that produces measurable results within the first 30 days.

Weeks 1-2: Assessment. Map the current production management workflow. Identify every system that holds production data: ERP, scheduling tools, spreadsheets, whiteboards, machine controllers. Document how the production manager currently tracks job status. Quantify the current OTD rate, the number of late deliveries per month, and the direct costs of late delivery (expediting, overtime, penalties). This assessment produces the baseline against which improvement is measured.

Weeks 2-4: Data connection. Connect to the ERP. Configure machine data collection for key equipment (start with the bottleneck machines, typically 8 to 12 machines that carry 70% of the production load). Set up the floor data input terminals. Validate that data flows correctly from all sources into the visibility platform.

Weeks 3-6: System build. Configure the dashboard views. Build the risk calculation engine using the shop's historical production data (the more history available, the more accurate the initial risk predictions). Set customer priority rules. Define alert thresholds. Create the interface the production management team will use daily.

Weeks 5-8: Pilot and refine. Run the system alongside existing processes for 2 to 3 weeks. Compare the system's risk predictions against actual outcomes. Refine the algorithms based on what the system gets right and wrong. Let the production team identify missing data points or interface improvements.

Weeks 7-10: Full deployment. Transition to the visibility system as the primary production management tool. Train all supervisors and lead personnel. Expand machine connectivity to remaining equipment. Establish the daily rhythm: production manager reviews the dashboard at shift start, addresses the risk queue, and verifies status at mid-shift.

The system improves continuously after deployment. Every completed job provides new data that refines the risk prediction models. Within 90 days, the system's delivery risk predictions typically reach 80% to 85% accuracy. Within 6 months, 90% or above. Getting Reliable Data from the Floor

The biggest implementation challenge is not technology. It is getting consistent data input from operators and supervisors on the shop floor. The system depends on knowing when jobs start, when operations complete, and when issues occur. If operators do not log these events, the system has blind spots.

Designing for the Floor

The input interface must be simple enough that an operator can log an event in under 10 seconds without removing their safety glasses. Barcode scanning is the most reliable method: the operator scans the job traveler barcode at operation start and operation complete. Two scans per operation, each taking 3 seconds.

Touchscreen terminals at work centers handle non-standard inputs: machine down, quality hold, waiting for material, setup in progress. The interface should have 6 to 8 large buttons covering 95% of events. No typing. No dropdown menus. No login screens. The terminal stays active and ready.

Tablet-based interfaces work for supervisors who move between work centers. They can update multiple job statuses, log material arrivals, and add notes without returning to a fixed terminal.

Building the Habit

The first 2 weeks of floor data collection determine long-term adoption. If operators see that their inputs produce visible results (a dashboard that reflects what they reported, a production manager who responds to the issues they flagged), compliance stays high. If the data disappears into a system nobody checks, operators stop logging within a month.

The production manager's daily use of the dashboard is the most powerful adoption driver. When an operator reports a machine down event and the production manager responds with a maintenance technician within 30 minutes because the system flagged it, the operator learns that their input matters. When a supervisor reports a material shortage and the purchasing department expedites within the hour because the visibility system showed the delivery impact, the supervisor reports the next shortage without being asked.

Metrics That Matter

Production visibility generates a large amount of data. The metrics that drive operational improvement are a focused subset.

On-time delivery rate. Measured against the original promise date, not the last revised date. This is the metric customers care about and the one most shops underreport. Target: 93% or above for general job shop work, 97% or above for high-consequence aerospace and medical customers.

Risk queue accuracy. What percentage of jobs flagged as at-risk actually ship late if no intervention occurs. This measures the quality of the system's predictions and improves over time as the model learns from your shop's specific patterns.

Recovery rate. What percentage of at-risk jobs are recovered to on-time status through intervention. A high recovery rate means the system is providing enough warning time for the production team to act. If jobs are being flagged but still shipping late, the warning is coming too late or the recovery resources are insufficient.

Equipment utilization (OEE). Overall Equipment Effectiveness measured as availability times performance times quality. Most job shops lack the data to calculate true OEE. The visibility system provides it automatically for every connected machine. Industry benchmark for job shop OEE is 60%. Most shops measure between 35% and 50% when the data is accurate.

Schedule adherence. How closely actual production follows the planned sequence. Low schedule adherence indicates either poor scheduling, frequent interruptions, or both. The visibility system identifies which causes drive the most deviation.

Average days late. For jobs that do ship late, how late are they on average. Reducing average late days from 5.2 to 2.1 represents a meaningful improvement in customer impact, even if OTD percentage has not yet reached target.

ROI: The Math on Visibility

Production visibility ROI comes from four sources, each measurable within the first 90 days of operation.

Direct Late Delivery Cost Reduction

Tally your expediting charges, premium freight costs, overtime for late order recovery, and contract penalties over the last 12 months. Most shops in the 50 to 200 employee range spend $120,000 to $400,000 annually on these costs. A visibility system that improves OTD from 82% to 91% typically reduces these costs by 40% to 60% in the first year.

Revenue Retention

Customer attrition from chronic delivery problems is the largest cost, and the hardest to measure precisely. One approach: identify customers whose order volume declined more than 20% year-over-year and investigate whether delivery performance was a factor. In manufacturing, a customer who leaves rarely announces the reason. They simply send fewer orders. For a shop with $8 million in annual revenue, losing 3% of revenue to delivery-driven customer attrition is $240,000 per year. Retaining even half of that through improved OTD more than justifies the visibility system investment.

Capacity Recovery

Better visibility into equipment utilization and production flow reveals capacity that was previously invisible. A shop that discovers 15% more usable capacity in its existing equipment can take on additional work without capital investment. On a shop floor with $3 million in CNC equipment, recovering 15% utilization is the functional equivalent of purchasing $450,000 in new machines.

Labor Efficiency

Production managers, supervisors, and planners spend 2 to 4 hours per day gathering status information: walking the floor, checking systems, making phone calls, attending status meetings. A visibility system that provides this information on a single screen recovers 50% to 70% of that time. For a shop with 3 people in production management roles, that is 3 to 6 hours per day of recovered management capacity. That time can be redirected to process improvement, customer communication, or capacity planning instead of status chasing.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do we need to replace our ERP?

No. The visibility system reads from your existing ERP without modifying it. Your ERP remains the system of record for orders, invoicing, and job costing. The visibility system adds an intelligence layer on top that your ERP was never designed to provide. Both systems run in parallel. For details on how AI tools integrate with common manufacturing ERPs, see our guide to AI quoting, which covers ERP integration in depth.

What if our machines are too old for data collection?

Retrofit sensors work on any machine with a power circuit, regardless of age. A 1990s Haas or Fadal without MTConnect capability can still be monitored for cycle status, idle time, and utilization through a $400 current sensor and a $200 wireless transmitter. The data is less granular than what a newer machine provides, but it covers the 80% that matters most: is the machine running, and if not, how long has it been idle.

How do we get operators to actually log data?

Design the input for the floor, not the office. Barcode scanning takes 3 seconds. Large touchscreen buttons take 5 seconds. No logins, no typing, no menus. The second requirement is visible results. When operators see that their inputs trigger action (maintenance response, material expediting, schedule adjustments), they log consistently. When their inputs disappear into a database nobody checks, they stop. The production manager's daily use of the system is the strongest driver of floor-level adoption.

How much does this cost?

A production visibility system for a shop with 30 to 150 employees and 15 to 40 machines typically costs $90,000 to $200,000 for the initial build, including ERP integration, machine connectivity, floor terminals, dashboard development, and the AI risk engine. Ongoing costs for system maintenance, model refinement, and expanding connectivity run $3,000 to $6,000 per month. Compare that to the cost of one lost customer or one year of expediting charges.

How fast will we see results?

The dashboard provides immediate value from the day it goes live, because the act of consolidating scattered data into a single view reveals problems that were previously invisible. Risk prediction accuracy improves over the first 90 days as the system accumulates data from your specific operation. Most shops report a measurable OTD improvement within the first 60 days, with the full impact realized by month 4 to 6 as the team integrates the system into their daily workflow.

Can this work for a shop that runs both production and job shop work?

Yes. The system handles mixed-mode manufacturing by applying different tracking and risk models to different job types. Repeat production jobs have predictable cycle times and established routings, so the system's predictions are highly accurate. First-run job shop work carries more variability, so the system applies wider risk margins. Both job types appear on the same dashboard, sorted and filtered however the production team prefers to view them.
