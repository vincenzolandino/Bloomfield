1943

The U.S. needs 300,000 aircraft to win a war. American manufacturers retool entire factories in months. Production schedules are handwritten on chalkboards. Tooling specs live in the memories of machinists who learned their trade as apprentices. The planes get built because the people on the floor know what they are doing.

2026

The parts are more precise. Five-axis machines cut tolerances that were impossible 20 years ago. But the quoting process still runs on tribal knowledge and spreadsheets. Critical specs still live in the heads of senior engineers. And 10,000 skilled machinists are retiring every year.

The manufacturers who built this country did it with expertise, precision, and an unwillingness to accept how things had always been done. That mindset is the reason AI matters now. The tooling has changed. The instinct is the same.

The data exists. The expertise exists. The systems to connect them do not.
