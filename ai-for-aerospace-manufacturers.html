The Problem

Aerospace manufacturing runs on documentation. The documentation runs on people.

The technical work is hard enough. The compliance burden on top of it is where shops lose time, margin, and experienced people who get tired of assembling the same cert packages by hand for the 500th time.

Cert packages assembled from 6 different systems

A single aerospace part ships with material certs, dimensional inspection reports, process certifications, test results, first-article documentation, and customer-specific paperwork. Your quality team pulls these from the ERP, the CMM software, the heat treat vendor, a shared drive, and email. One missing document holds an entire shipment. The average cert package at a 100-person aerospace shop takes 3 to 5 hours to compile by hand.

First-article inspections that repeat avoidable work

An AS9102 first-article inspection report requires ballooned drawings, dimensional results mapped to each characteristic, material traceability, and process verification. When a similar part ran 18 months ago, the quality engineer built a nearly identical FAI. That work lives in a folder nobody can find or a file named something nobody remembers. The next FAI starts from scratch. Compliance prep is where aerospace shops lose the most recoverable hours.

Quoting that underestimates compliance cost

Aerospace quoting requires estimating raw material with long procurement lead times, tight-tolerance machining across multiple operations, special process outsourcing for NADCAP-approved heat treat or NDT, and the quality overhead for documentation at every step. Estimators who forget to account for the 6 hours of cert prep or the 14-week titanium lead time erode margin before the job hits the floor. Quoting speed and accuracy determine whether aerospace shops grow or stagnate.

Process knowledge concentrated in a few people

Your senior quality manager knows which NADCAP vendor handles Inconel 718 heat treat within the temperature tolerance your prime contractor requires. Your lead machinist knows the fixturing approach that holds +/- 0.0002" on a turbine blade root form. ITAR restrictions mean you cannot send drawings to offshore teams for help. When these people leave, the knowledge gap is immediate and expensive.
