Why Manufacturing Quoting Takes So Long

AI-powered quoting for manufacturers is a system that uses historical job data, material pricing, and machine capacity to compress the RFQ-to-quote cycle from days to hours. The system gives estimators instant access to every comparable past job the shop has run, including actual costs, winning margins, and quality issues, so they can build accurate quotes faster.

The quoting process at a typical 50-person job shop works like this. An RFQ arrives by email. The estimator opens the drawing, reads the specs, and begins a manual search through memory and past records. Which jobs were similar to this one. What material did we use. How long did it actually take on the lathe versus what we estimated. Did we have quality issues with this customer. What margin held.

That search happens across multiple systems. The ERP has some of the history. Spreadsheets have the pricing models. The estimator's memory fills in the gaps. A single experienced estimator might handle 15 to 25 quotes per day. If they are out sick, on vacation, or retire, the shop's quoting capacity drops by half or more.

For a precision machining shop quoting complex multi-operation parts in Inconel or titanium, a single quote can take 4 to 8 hours of estimator time. Simpler parts in aluminum or mild steel might take 45 minutes to 2 hours. Multiply that across 300 to 500 RFQs per month and the bottleneck becomes clear. The shop's revenue ceiling is set by how fast one or two people can process incoming work.

The Information Retrieval Problem

The estimator is not slow. The information retrieval is slow. Finding the three most relevant past jobs in a system with 15,000 historical records requires either a remarkable memory or a lot of clicking. Checking current material prices against what was paid six months ago on a similar job means opening a different system. Reviewing the customer's order history to understand their tolerance for lead time versus price means yet another system or set of emails.

The estimator's expertise lies in interpreting this information and making the judgment call on price, lead time, and risk. The 60% to 70% of their time spent finding and assembling that information is where the waste sits.

The Hidden Cost of Slow Quotes

Manufacturers that respond to RFQs within two business days win approximately 35% of competitive bids. Those that take five or more days win 12%. The data comes from industry surveys across job shops in the $5 million to $50 million revenue range, and the pattern is consistent: speed correlates with win rate more strongly than price within a 10% band.

The math on this is uncomfortable. A shop quoting 400 RFQs per month with an average job value of $7,200 and a 15% win rate books $432,000 per month. Moving to a 22% win rate by cutting response time produces $633,600 per month. That is $2.4 million in annual revenue sitting in the quoting queue, waiting for someone to find the right historical job record.

The Costs You Cannot See on a P&L

Slow quoting creates downstream costs that rarely get attributed back to the quoting process. Estimators under pressure to move faster make margin errors. A $45,000 aerospace job quoted at 22% margin instead of 30% because the estimator missed a secondary operation costs $3,600 in margin erosion on a single job. Scale that across 10 misquoted jobs per month and the annual impact is $430,000 in margin left on the table.

Then there is the customer experience. A buyer at a Tier 1 aerospace supplier sending RFQs to five shops awards work to the one that responds quickly, accurately, and completely. The shops that take a week to respond often do not get a second chance. The RFQ pipeline narrows quietly, and the shop attributes the decline to "the market" rather than the quoting process.

Knowledge concentration is the third hidden cost. When 80% of quoting expertise lives in one person's head, the business carries a single point of failure with no backup, no redundancy, and no succession plan. If that person leaves, the shop's quoting accuracy and speed degrade for 6 to 12 months while a replacement gets up to speed.

What an AI Quoting System Actually Does

An AI quoting system is a software tool that indexes a manufacturer's historical job data, connects it to current material pricing and machine capacity, and presents the estimator with a structured reference package for every new RFQ. The estimator does not stop making decisions. They start making them with complete information.

When a new RFQ arrives, the system reads the part description, material, tolerances, quantities, and any customer-specific requirements. It matches these parameters against the shop's indexed job history and retrieves the most comparable past jobs. Each match includes the actual cost breakdown, the margin that won, the machine routing, the actual run time versus estimated, any quality issues, and whether the customer accepted the original quote or negotiated.

The Five Outputs

Similar job matches. The system surfaces 3 to 8 past jobs ranked by similarity, with full cost breakdowns. An estimator quoting a 4-axis machined bracket in 304 stainless sees every similar bracket the shop has run, how it was routed, what it actually cost, and whether the margin held.

Material cost comparison. Current pricing for the specified material from your suppliers, compared against what you paid on recent comparable jobs. If 6061-T6 aluminum bar stock has increased 14% since the last time you quoted this part family, the system flags it.

Customer history. Every past interaction with this customer: jobs completed, quotes accepted and declined, payment history, quality rejections, and any documented preferences. A customer who consistently pushes back on lead time but accepts pricing within 5% of your initial quote requires a different strategy than one who negotiates aggressively on price.

Risk flags. Parts or parameters that have caused problems. If tight-tolerance bore work on this material has produced scrap rates above 4% historically, the system flags it so the estimator can build contingency into the price or adjust the routing.

Suggested pricing range. Based on historical data, the system calculates a pricing range with margin scenarios. The estimator sees what a 25%, 30%, and 35% margin looks like, informed by what the customer has accepted in the past and what the market will bear based on recent win/loss patterns.

Anatomy of an AI-Assisted Quote

Here is what the process looks like after implementation, step by step.

8:15 AM. An RFQ arrives from a customer requesting 200 pieces of a machined housing in 17-4 PH stainless, with GD&T callouts and an ITAR requirement. The estimator opens the AI quoting system.

8:17 AM. The system has already parsed the RFQ (which arrived by email), identified the material, quantity, and key features, and retrieved four comparable past jobs from the last three years. Job 2847 is the closest match: same material, similar geometry, 150-piece lot, completed eight months ago with a 28% margin and zero rejections.

8:22 AM. The estimator reviews the matches, notes that material pricing has increased 9% since Job 2847, checks the current machine schedule for capacity, and adjusts the routing to account for a tooling improvement made since the last run. The system generates the quote document with all supporting data.

8:35 AM. The quote is sent. Total elapsed time: 20 minutes. The same quote, without the system, would have taken 3 to 4 hours of searching, calculating, and assembling.

The estimator's judgment is present at every step. The system did not generate the quote autonomously. It assembled the information, presented it in a structured format, and let the estimator apply their expertise to the decision. That is the distinction between an AI tool and an AI replacement. The estimator is faster and better informed. They are not removed from the process.

Data Requirements

An AI quoting system needs three categories of data to function. Most shops have two of the three readily available and can assemble the third within a few weeks.

Category 1: Historical Job Data

This is the foundation. Every job your shop has run, with as much detail as your ERP captured. At minimum, the system needs: part number or description, material, quantity, machine routing, estimated hours, actual hours, material cost, total job cost, selling price, customer name, and completion date.

The more fields available, the better the matching algorithm performs. Secondary operations, surface treatments, inspection requirements, and packaging specs all improve match accuracy. A shop with 3,000 or more historical jobs in its ERP has enough data for a highly effective system. Shops with 1,000 to 3,000 jobs can build a useful system that improves as new jobs are completed.

Category 2: Material and Supplier Data

Current material pricing, supplier lead times, and minimum order quantities. If your shop maintains a material pricing spreadsheet (most do), that is sufficient for the initial build. The system can also connect to supplier portals or material cost databases for real-time pricing where available.

Category 3: Quote History

Past quotes, including the ones that did not convert. This data teaches the system what price points win and lose for different customers, part types, and quantities. Many shops track this in their ERP. Others track it in spreadsheets or email folders. Both work. The key data points are: what was quoted, what was the final price, did the customer accept, and if not, was there feedback on why.

Data Quality

Your data does not need to be clean in a database-administrator sense. It needs to be consistent enough for the system to match on. If 60% of your job records have complete material specifications and the other 40% use abbreviated descriptions, the system can handle that. It learns the patterns in your naming conventions, abbreviations, and data entry habits. The initial data preparation phase identifies gaps and addresses the ones that matter most for matching accuracy.

ERP Integration: JobBOSS, Epicor, ProShop, and Others

The quoting system connects to your ERP through data exports, direct database connections, or API integrations, depending on which system you run and how it is deployed.

JobBOSS / E2 Shop System

JobBOSS stores job history in a SQL Server database. The quoting system connects through a read-only database link or scheduled CSV exports. Most JobBOSS installations have 5 to 15 years of job history available. The integration typically takes 1 to 2 weeks, including data mapping and validation. E2 Shop System (now part of the same ECI family) uses a similar data structure.

Epicor (Kinetic / Vantage)

Epicor provides REST APIs in its Kinetic version and ODBC access for older Vantage installations. The data model is more complex than JobBOSS, with separate tables for job headers, operations, materials, and labor. Integration takes 2 to 3 weeks. Epicor's multi-company and multi-site capabilities add complexity for shops that operate across locations.

ProShop ERP

ProShop is a browser-based ERP built specifically for job shops and contract manufacturers. It provides API access and has a well-documented data model. ProShop's advantage for AI integration is that it captures more granular process data than most ERPs, including detailed setup notes and first-article inspection records. Integration typically takes 1 to 2 weeks.

Global Shop Solutions

Global Shop stores data in a Progress database, which requires specific export procedures. The system captures comprehensive job costing data that works well for quoting applications. Integration takes 2 to 3 weeks, with the export configuration being the primary variable.

Other Systems

Shops running IQMS (now DELMIAworks), MIE Trak, Infor VISUAL, Plex, or any other ERP can integrate through data exports. If the ERP can produce a CSV or Excel file of job history, the quoting system can ingest it. The integration is less automated than a direct connection, but the functionality is identical.

ROI Calculation

Quoting ROI comes from three measurable sources: increased win rate from faster response, recovered estimator capacity, and reduced margin erosion from quoting errors.

Win Rate Improvement

Use your own numbers. Take your current monthly quote volume, average job value, and win rate. Model a 4 to 8 percentage point improvement in win rate, which is the typical range for shops that cut response time from 4+ days to under 2 days.

Example: 350 quotes per month, $6,800 average value, 16% current win rate = $380,800 monthly revenue. At 22% win rate = $523,600 monthly. Annual improvement: $1.7 million. Even at half that improvement, the system pays for itself in the first quarter.

Estimator Capacity

If the quoting system reduces the information-gathering phase by 60% to 70%, an estimator who currently handles 18 quotes per day can handle 28 to 32. That is either the capacity to quote more work without hiring, or the bandwidth for the estimator to spend more time on complex, high-value quotes that require detailed engineering review.

One additional full-time estimator costs $75,000 to $95,000 annually in salary and benefits. If the AI system provides the equivalent of 0.5 to 1.0 additional FTE of quoting capacity, that savings alone covers a substantial portion of the implementation cost.

Margin Accuracy

Track your quoted margin versus actual margin on completed jobs. Most shops find a 2% to 5% gap between what they expected to make and what they actually made. Part of that gap comes from estimating errors: missed operations, outdated material prices, incorrect cycle time assumptions. An AI system that surfaces actual historical costs for comparable jobs narrows that gap. On $5 million in annual revenue, a 2% margin improvement is $100,000 straight to the bottom line.

Implementation Timeline

A complete AI quoting system implementation follows a defined sequence. The timeline below is based on a shop running 20 to 150 employees with an established ERP containing at least two years of job history.

Weeks 1-2: Discovery and data audit. Map the current quoting workflow. Interview the estimating team. Document what data exists, where it lives, and what format it takes. Define the success metrics: target response time, target win rate improvement, target margin accuracy.

Weeks 2-4: Data preparation. Export historical job data from the ERP. Clean and structure the records. Identify and resolve data quality issues (missing material specs, inconsistent naming, duplicate records). Organize supplementary data sources (material pricing sheets, customer files, quote archives).

Weeks 3-7: System build. Develop the matching algorithm trained on your specific data. Build the user interface the estimator will use daily. Create the ERP integration layer. Configure the material pricing connection. Build the quote output templates.

Weeks 6-9: Testing. Run the system alongside the existing process. Compare AI-assisted quotes against manual quotes for accuracy and speed. Let the estimating team use the system on real RFQs and provide feedback. Refine matching accuracy. Adjust the interface based on how the team actually works.

Weeks 8-10: Deployment. Transition to the AI system as the primary quoting tool. Train any team members who were not involved in the testing phase. Establish the feedback loop: when the estimator overrides a system suggestion, that data improves the next recommendation.

Total timeline: 8 to 10 weeks from kickoff to full deployment. Some shops with cleaner data and simpler ERP integrations have completed implementations in 6 weeks. Complex multi-site operations with multiple ERP instances may take 12 to 14 weeks.

What Changes for the Estimator

The estimator's job changes from information retrieval to information evaluation. Before the system, 60% to 70% of the estimator's time goes to finding data: searching the ERP, reviewing old quotes, checking material prices, pulling up customer history. After the system, that information is assembled and presented before the estimator begins their review.

What does not change: the estimator still reads the drawing, evaluates manufacturability, selects the routing, makes the margin decision, and manages the customer relationship. The system does not replace judgment. It eliminates the manual data assembly that consumes most of the estimator's day.

Experienced estimators typically embrace the system fastest, because they understand how much time they spend looking for information they know exists somewhere. A veteran estimator with 20 years of experience who can finally search "what did we quote for Pratt & Whitney on that titanium bracket two years ago" and get an answer in seconds is a person whose expertise just became 3x more productive.

Training New Estimators

The system also changes how new estimators learn. Instead of spending 12 to 18 months building the mental database of past jobs that an experienced estimator carries, a new estimator has access to the full institutional memory from day one. They still need to develop the judgment to interpret it, but the learning curve on information retrieval drops from years to weeks. For shops where the lead estimator is approaching retirement, this is an urgency, not a convenience. What AI Quoting Cannot Do

Honesty about limitations builds trust and prevents implementation disappointments.

AI cannot quote a part type you have never made. The matching algorithm works by comparing new RFQs against your historical data. If a customer requests a process or material you have never worked with, the system has no reference data to draw from. The estimator must quote from first principles, as they always have.

AI cannot replace engineering review. A complex part with tight GD&T callouts, exotic materials, or novel geometry requires an engineer or senior estimator to evaluate manufacturability. The AI system can surface similar past challenges, but the go/no-go decision on a new capability remains a human judgment.

AI cannot fix bad data. If your ERP has 5,000 jobs but 3,000 of them have no material cost recorded, the system can only work with what exists. Data quality improves over time as new jobs are completed with better record-keeping, but the system will not fabricate historical data to fill gaps.

AI cannot manage the customer relationship. Pricing strategy, negotiation tactics, and the decision to accept work below target margin to build a relationship with a strategic account: these are human decisions that require context no system can fully capture. The AI provides the data. The estimator provides the strategy.

Build vs. Buy

Manufacturers evaluating AI quoting have three options: build custom software, buy an off-the-shelf quoting platform, or integrate AI capabilities into their existing ERP.

Off-the-Shelf Platforms

Several vendors sell manufacturing quoting software with AI features. These platforms work best for shops with standard processes (turning, milling, sheet metal) and straightforward part types. Pricing typically runs $1,500 to $5,000 per month. The limitation is flexibility. If your shop's quoting process involves custom calculations, industry-specific requirements (ITAR, AS9100, NADCAP), or unusual machine capabilities, an off-the-shelf platform may not accommodate them without significant customization.

ERP Add-ons

Some ERP vendors offer quoting enhancement modules. These are convenient because they share the same database, but they are constrained by the ERP's data model and interface. The AI capabilities in these modules are typically basic: simple cost estimation based on historical averages rather than sophisticated matching and analysis.

Custom Build

Custom AI quoting software is built around your specific data, workflows, and business rules. It integrates with your ERP without replacing it. The upfront cost is higher ($75,000 to $200,000 depending on scope), but the tool is yours. It reflects how your estimating team works, handles the edge cases specific to your operation, and improves over time with your data. For shops where quoting is a competitive advantage, custom is the right answer because the tool becomes part of the operational advantage that competitors cannot replicate.

The choice depends on complexity. A 20-person shop doing standard CNC work can start with an off-the-shelf platform and evaluate custom later. A 100-person aerospace shop with ITAR requirements, multi-operation routings, and customer-specific pricing structures needs custom from the start. Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is the AI compared to an experienced estimator?

The AI does not compete with the estimator. It supports the estimator. Accuracy is measured by whether the estimator produces better quotes faster with the system than without it. In testing across multiple implementations, estimators using the AI system reduce quoting errors by 30% to 45% and produce quotes in one-third the time. The system finds relevant past jobs that the estimator would have missed, especially in shops with large job histories where no single person remembers every job.

What happens when material prices change rapidly?

The system connects to your material pricing data, whether that is a maintained spreadsheet, a supplier portal, or a material cost database. When prices change, the next quote reflects the current number. The system also flags when the current price for a material deviates more than a set threshold (typically 8% to 12%) from the price used on comparable past jobs, alerting the estimator to adjust their margin expectations.

Can it handle multi-operation parts with outside processing?

Yes. The system models the full routing: internal operations across multiple machines, outside processing (heat treat, plating, coating, grinding), inspection holds, and assembly. Each operation carries its own cost model, and the system matches each one against historical actuals. Outside processing costs are tracked by vendor, process type, and turnaround time.

What about ITAR and security requirements?

AI quoting systems for manufacturers handling ITAR-controlled work are deployed in secure, U.S.-hosted environments with access controls, encryption at rest and in transit, and audit logging. Your technical data does not leave your security boundary. Ensure any vendor you evaluate can demonstrate compliance with the specific security requirements your contracts mandate.

How does the system handle rush orders?

Rush orders require the same data as standard quotes, with the addition of current machine capacity and schedule. The system checks open capacity across your equipment and shifts, calculates the overtime or schedule disruption cost of accelerating the job, and adds those costs to the standard estimate. The estimator can adjust the rush premium based on the customer relationship and strategic value of the order.
